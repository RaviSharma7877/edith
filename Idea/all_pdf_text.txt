Combined Feature Inventory for an Accounting-First 
BusinessOS Platform 
Executive summary 
This report inventories the combined platform in the order you requested: first the 
accounting system modeled on core TallyPrime-like capabilities, then the broader 
BusinessOS AI feature set layered around it. Team size, budget, primary country, payment 
rails, deployment regions, and AI model vendors are unspecified. That is strategically 
important because accounting scope changes materially with jurisdiction, tax rules, bank 
feeds, and filing obligations; likewise, AI vendor choice changes cost, privacy, and model-
governance controls. 
The recommended platform shape is an accounting-first modular monolith with strong 
tenant isolation and an evented integration layer. The accounting core should be treated as 
the system of record: invoices, bills, payments, journal entries, bank reconciliation, taxes, 
ledgers, and reports. BusinessOS AI should sit around that core as the system of 
engagement: CRM, website/CMS, forms, projects, files, proposals, portal, automations, 
analytics, and AI assistance. The reason is operational rather than stylistic: accounting 
needs immutable posting, maker-checker approval, period locks, reconciliation, and 
auditability; business workflows need speed, flexibility, and cross-object automation. 
TallyPrime’s official help emphasizes voucher-based accounting, bank reconciliation, GST 
reconciliation, and e-invoicing/returns, while ERPNext, Odoo, Akaunting, and LedgerSMB 
all center their accounting stacks around chart of accounts, journals, reconciliation, taxes, 
and financial reporting. [1]

Complete UI Specification for BusinessOS AI and a
TallyPrime-like Accounting SaaS
Executive summary
This report defines a complete no-code UI specification for two products: BusinessOS AI, a multi-tenant
work operating system with marketing, website publishing, CRM, work execution, invoicing, analytics, and
AI assistance; and a TallyPrime-like multi-tenant Accounting SaaS, focused on chart of accounts,
journals, invoicing, payments, tax, reconciliation, reporting, and auditability. Team size, budget, primary
country, payment rails, deployment regions, and AI model vendors are unspecified and are treated as such
throughout.
The core design recommendation is to build one shared design system and two product shells. Both
products should share the same black-background/white-text visual language, layout primitives, data-entry
patterns, accessibility baseline, keyboard model, and admin/developer/help portals. They should diverge at
the workflow layer: BusinessOS AI needs more breadth, exploratory navigation, dashboards, card/list/
canvas experiences, and an AI copilot woven into multiple surfaces; the accounting product needs more 
depth, denser forms and tables, stronger confirmation flows, immutable posting cues, reconciliation
workspaces, and reporting states that optimize correctness over speed.
The visual and interaction baseline in this report is intentionally stricter than “looks good in dark mode.” It is
designed to satisfy the practical expectations of the World Wide Web Consortium’s WCAG 2.2 guidance
for contrast, keyboard access, focus visibility, target size, status messages, and error prevention, while
following the accessible component semantics documented in WAI-ARIA Authoring Practices for dialogs,
tabs, grids, comboboxes, and breadcrumbs. It also aligns with official dark-mode and touch-target guidance
from Apple and accessible color/touch-target guidance from Google’s Material Design
documentation. In practice, that means: visible 2px focus rings, minimum 44px touch targets for primary
controls, 48px for navigation-heavy and mobile-primary controls, confirmation/reversal patterns for
financial actions, and accessible status messages that do not rely only on toasts or color . 
For delivery, the cleanest path is a single component library, ten reusable layout templates, and page-
level specs expressed as route inventories. That lets a human design team, an AI UI generator , or both
produce high-fidelity screens consistently. The estimated UI-only effort is lower for BusinessOS AI than for
the accounting product, but the BusinessOS surface area is broader . The accounting product has fewer
page archetypes overall, yet significantly higher detail density and more state complexity around posting,
reconciliation, and reports. As a result, BusinessOS AI is broader and more exploratory; Accounting SaaS is
narrower but more exacting and audit-sensitive.

Signup and onboarding
BusinessOS AI wireframe:
Desktop uses T-AuthSplit: left 40% brand/value panel; right 60% task form. After account creation, the
user lands on Workspace Select, then a T-Wizard onboarding flow with 5–7 steps: company basics,
goals, key modules, first site/project/CRM import, branding, invite team, done. At the top there is a 72px
progress header with current step, percent complete, and “save and finish later .” Bottom actions are back/
continue, never more than two primary CTAs visible.
Accounting SaaS wireframe:
Same shell, but company creation starts earlier . The wizard steps are: company profile, fiscal year and
currency, country/tax mode, chart-of-accounts template, opening balances/import choice, invite users,
complete. This flow uses more inline help panels and examples than BusinessOS AI, because domain
correctness matters more than fast completion.
Create website and publish
Pages and progression:
/app/sites → /app/sites/:siteId → /app/sites/:siteId/editor → /app/sites/:siteId/
pages/:pageId/settings → /app/sites/:siteId/publish.
Wireframe structure:
The editor is a three-rail layout:
Left rail: page tree, section library, assets
Center canvas: live page preview at desktop/tablet/mobile widths
Right rail: selected block/page/theme properties
The top bar contains page name, draft status, preview toggle, undo/redo, AI generate, save, publish. The
bottom bar is optional and only appears during responsive preview. On tablet, only one side rail is open at a
time; on mobile, the canvas goes full width and both rails are drawers.
Critical micro-interactions:
Selected block gets an accent outline and floating context actions.
Unsaved state appears as a persistent “Draft unsaved” pill, not just a toast.
Publish CTA opens a two-step drawer: preflight summary, then success view with live URL and
rollback link.
DNS verification uses inline progress with last-checked timestamp, not just animated dots.

Final recommendation
If both products must share one visual language, do not force them to share one interaction style. The right
approach is:
One token system
One component library
One accessibility contract
Two workflow grammars
BusinessOS AI should feel exploratory, compositional, and assistant-friendly.
The accounting product should feel controlled, legible, confirmable, and audit-safe.
That split produces the most coherent black-and-white UI system while still respecting the fundamentally
different cognitive load of a work operating system versus an accounting system.

review/post and period close. Official docs from Akaunting, Odoo, ERPNext, and 
LedgerSMB all point to the same persistent workflow spine: chart of accounts, journal 
entry, tax, bank reconciliation, and financial reporting. [6]

Free-First SaaS Build Plan for BusinessOS AI and an
Accounting System as a Service
Executive summary
This report treats BusinessOS AI as the prior in-conversation brief: a multi-module operating system for
service businesses that unifies CRM, projects, proposals, invoicing, client collaboration, knowledge retrieval,
automations, analytics, and an AI copilot. It treats the second product as a TallyPrime-like multi-tenant
accounting SaaS focused on core bookkeeping and compliance workflows rather than the full breadth of
desktop ERP features. Team size, budget, primary country, payment rails, bank-feed providers, deployment
regions, data-residency requirements, and exact AI model vendors are unspecified. That matters because
those unknowns affect pricing, compliance scope, and the right tenancy model.
The two products should not be built the same way. BusinessOS AI is well suited to a pooled modular
monolith with tenant-scoped data and PostgreSQL Row-Level Security, plus an optional bridge path for
premium isolated tenants. The accounting SaaS should start with a bridge model: a shared control plane
for auth, onboarding, billing, and support, but stronger isolation for ledger data, ideally schema-per-
tenant or database-per-tenant for the accounting core. This recommendation follows the SaaS tenancy
patterns documented by Amazon Web Services and the database-per-tenant guidance published by
Neon , while relying on PostgreSQL RLS for any pooled tables that remain shared. 
The economic picture is favorable for prototyping and early MVP work. A real prototype can run at
effectively $0 platform spend if it stays inside free tiers such as Cloudflare Pages, GitHub Free, Neon Free,
Supabase Free, Resend Free, PostHog Free, and Figma Starter . However , once background jobs, long-lived
workers, PDF rendering, bank imports, tax reports, and production-grade observability become mandatory,
“free-only” usually stops being realistic. The practical interpretation of “free/open-source first” is therefore: 
use free managed tiers where they are generous, and self-host only the pieces that create strategic
leverage or materially reduce variable cost. 
The effort profiles are also asymmetric. A focused 60-day BusinessOS AI MVP is achievable in roughly 32–40
person-weeks if AI scope is disciplined and accounting remains light. A credible accounting MVP with
double-entry integrity, reporting, tax scaffolding, and reconciliation is more realistically 40–52 person-
weeks, even before multi-country tax localization. The reason is not UI complexity; it is the need for
immutable journal logic, strong auditability, period locks, reconciliation, and localization boundaries.
TallyPrime’s own workflows around chart of accounts, voucher types, payments, bank reconciliation, GST
reconciliation, and e-Way Bill handling illustrate how quickly accounting scope expands once compliance
enters the product surface.

Accounting SaaS scope
For the accounting product, the MVP should emulate the core accounting spine rather than the whole

Architecture and data models
The architectural principle that should govern both products is simple: monolith first, isolation where risk
justifies it, and asynchronous boundaries around non-transactional work. That means one deployable
application initially, but not one flat data model.
Recommended tenancy model
For pooled systems, PostgreSQL RLS is an important control because it restricts rows per-role, defaults to
deny when enabled without policies, and supports command-specific policies. It is powerful enough for
most BusinessOS AI tables if the application never connects with owner or BYPASSRLS roles. For
Accounting, however , RLS alone is not enough as the sole boundary for ledger data because support
workflows, customer restores, noisy-neighbor behavior , and jurisdiction-based storage tend to create
operational pressure toward stronger tenant isolation. That is why the recommended accounting model is a
bridge: pooled control-plane data plus more strongly isolated financial data.

Executive summary
The uploaded BusinessOS AI brief defines an extremely broad “system-as-a-service” product: website
builder and deployer , CRM, marketing automation, analytics, support, operations, team productivity,
inventory, admin, developer APIs, and a cross-cutting AI layer . Treated literally, that is not a 60-day MVP; it is
a multi-year product portfolio. The right interpretation is to build a modular monolith with workers, ship
a narrow commercial wedge first, and reserve the rest for staged expansion. For BusinessOS AI, the best
wedge is: multi-tenant auth + onboarding + CRM + website/CMS + deployment + analytics + AI
assistant + basic admin/billing. For a Telly Prime-like product, the best wedge is: video upload + asset
library + transcoding + HLS/DASH playback + embed player + analytics, with live streaming as a
controlled beta, not a day-one commitment. That recommendation aligns with the scope in the BusinessOS
brief and with the operational reality of modern frontend, backend, auth, storage, queueing, observability,
and media infrastructures. 
For the web stack, the strongest free/open-source default is Next.js + Tailwind CSS + shadcn/ui on the
frontend, NestJS for the main API, PostgreSQL + Prisma for system-of-record data, Auth.js for lightweight
app auth or Keycloak/ZITADEL for enterprise identity, BullMQ + Redis for background jobs, MinIO or
Cloudflare R2 for object storage, PostHog + Metabase/Matomo + Grafana LGTM for analytics and
observability, and LiteLLM + Ollama/vLLM + Langfuse for the AI layer . Those choices minimize lock-in
while keeping a clean modern UI path and preserving an enterprise migration story. 
For the video product, the recommended free/open-source emulation of managed platforms is: tus/tusd
for resumable ingestion, FFmpeg for transcoding, Shaka Packager for HLS/DASH packaging, MediaMTX or

enterprise OTT parity.

Product scope and module strategy
The BusinessOS AI brief is best understood as a platform of platforms. If built naively, its biggest risk is
not engineering failure but surface-area dilution: too many half-complete modules, weak data quality, and
no compelling first workflow. The correct scope cut is to define a common tenant layer and then choose the
modules that compound user value in one motion: capture demand, publish presence, track
relationships, measure outcomes, and automate next actions. That is why the recommended

Analytics + CMS/Website Builder + Deployer + CRM + AI Assistant + Admin/Plan Management.
Marketing automation, support, finance, productivity, inventory, and marketplace/developer expansions
should be layered after the control plane is stable.

analytics, player/embed, tenant/admin, API/webhooks, and billing/usage metering. Live is the most
operationally fragile module because it couples encoder behavior , protocol negotiation, transcoding

Architecture, tenancy, data model, and API design
The best starting architecture for both products is a modular monolith plus workers. For BusinessOS AI,
that means one application repository and one main API process, organized into modules with clear
boundaries and an outbox/event pattern. For Telly-like products, it means one control plane monolith plus 
separate stateless worker pools for ingest, transcode, packaging, thumbnailing, and analytics
enrichment. Do not split either product into many microservices before you have operational evidence.
Even the AWS SaaS guidance emphasizes choosing from pool, bridge, and silo models based on isolation
and operational needs, not ideology. 
The tenancy recommendation is different by product. BusinessOS AI should begin with pool + bridge:
shared app tier , shared database, strict tenant_id scoping everywhere, row-level security where
practical, and then separate databases or schemas for premium/regulated tenants. Telly-like platforms
should start with a bridge model: shared control plane, shared analytics infrastructure, but storage buckets,
playback keys, rate limits, and optionally separate databases for higher tiers. That split keeps the control
plane efficient while creating a path to enterprise isolation later . PostgreSQL row security, Supabase’s RLS
guidance, and Neon’s database-per-tenant design all support this progression.

Security, compliance, and AI safety controls
The baseline security standard should be OWASP ASVS-aligned, with specific attention to the current
OWASP Top 10 and the OWASP GenAI risk taxonomy for AI-enabled systems. That means secure defaults in
auth flows, tenant isolation, secrets management, input validation, auditability, supply chain hygiene, and
defensive output handling. For AI features, the two biggest practical risks are prompt injection and 
sensitive information disclosure, so the AI layer must be treated as a semi-trusted subsystem—not an
omnipotent administrator . 
At the application layer , use Auth.js for simple app-native auth or Keycloak/ZITADEL for enterprise SSO,
MFA, and identity-provider federation. At the database layer , use tenant-scoped queries everywhere and
enable PostgreSQL row-level security for the most sensitive shared tables. At the infrastructure layer ,
enforce TLS end to end, prefer “Full (strict)” style origin verification, and publish explicit security headers
including CSP , X-Frame-Options, X-Content-Type-Options, Permissions-Policy, and Referrer-Policy. Those are
table stakes, not premium features. 
For compliance, do not promise certifications you do not yet hold. In the first year , the realistic target is to
be compliance-ready by architecture: data classification, consent records, retention controls, export/
delete flows, DPA templates, region-aware deployment choices, and per-tenant audit trails. For AI
governance, align process design with the NIST AI RMF and GenAI profile: identify risks, document model
routing and prompt policies, test with evaluations and red-teaming, and maintain human override for
material business actions.

Feature gating and pricing strategy
The pricing model should follow cost causality. For BusinessOS AI, the expensive units are seats, contacts,
automations, storage, domains, background-job execution, and AI usage. For Telly-like products, the
expensive units are storage, processing minutes, delivery minutes, concurrent live channels, and analytics
retention. Managed infrastructure pricing already reflects this logic: serverless databases charge on active
compute/storage, object stores charge on storage and operations, and managed video platforms charge on
stored and delivered video. Your pricing should mirror the same hard-cost vectors.

Migration path and side-by-side analysis
The migration path should be staged by scaling boundary, not by module vanity. For BusinessOS AI, split
services only after you can prove that one module has fundamentally different availability or throughput
needs. The first likely breakouts are deployment/build workers, event ingestion/analytics, AI gateway,
and support/messaging adapters. For Telly-like products, split earlier where the scaling physics are
already known: ingest, transcoding, packaging, playback token service, and analytics ingestion should
be separable even if the public API remains unified. 
Enterprise features should also follow a predictable ladder . First come governance features—SSO, audit
logs, retention policy, data export/delete, custom domains, and webhooks. Then come isolation features—
bridge/silo tenancy, dedicated databases, VPC/private networking, IP allow lists, and region pinning. Only
after that should you consider heavyweight options such as marketplace ecosystems, white-label partner
control planes, private AI inference, or advanced video rights controls. That ordering preserves commercial
leverage while avoiding architecture overreach.

Open questions and limitations
The biggest research limitation is naming, not architecture: the exact “Telly Prime” product could not be
confirmed from primary indexed sources, so the comparison uses the feature profile you specified rather
than a verified vendor specification. Searches for the term surfaced unrelated TallyPrime accounting
material instead of a primary video SaaS page. 
The second limitation is platform economics: fully free/open-source tools can credibly emulate much of a
commercial video platform’s pipeline, but they do not fully replace a global CDN, commercial DRM/rights
stack, or the operational convenience of an all-in-one managed video API. If you need true enterprise OTT
behavior , plan for managed infrastructure or premium self-hosting spend later , even if the MVP begins on
open tools.
